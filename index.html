Market Theme Research Note

The Power Problem:
AI Infrastructure's $3 Trillion Supercycle

Data centers are no longer a tech story. They are an energy, real estate, and industrial story—and the constraint has shifted from silicon to the power grid.

Thematic Conviction: Structurally Bullish with Execution Risk

Overview

What Is the AI Infrastructure Theme?

AI infrastructure encompasses the physical layer that makes artificial intelligence possible at scale: data centers, power generation, cooling systems, network connectivity, and the specialized chips inside them. It is the picks-and-shovels play on the AI gold rush—the companies supplying the infrastructure tend to win regardless of which AI model or application ultimately dominates.

🏗️ Three Layers of the Stack

Compute: GPUs (NVIDIA), custom AI chips (Google TPUs, AWS Trainium), high-bandwidth memory (SK Hynix HBM).

Facilities: Hyperscale data centers (AWS, Azure, Google), colocation operators (Equinix, Digital Realty), and specialized AI-campus developers.

Power & Cooling: Utilities, transformer manufacturers (ABB, Eaton), liquid cooling specialists, and nuclear energy suppliers — the fastest-growing sub-layer in 2026.

For most of 2022–2024, the bottleneck was silicon: too few GPUs, too long lead times. That constraint has migrated. The new chokepoint is power infrastructure—and unlike chips, you cannot fab a new electrical grid in 12 months.

Timeliness

Why Does This Theme Matter Right Now?

We are in the middle of what JLL calls an investment supercycle — a once-in-a-generation infrastructure buildout. The numbers are staggering and accelerating, not moderating. Three structural forces are compressing timelines:

1. Inference is replacing training as the dominant workload. Training an AI model is a one-time event. Running it millions of times daily (inference) creates permanent, compounding demand. By 2030, inference is projected to account for 75% of all AI compute. Every successful AI product deployed today locks in years of infrastructure consumption.

2. Rack power densities have crossed a physical threshold. The NVIDIA H100 GPU drew ~700W. The current-generation GB200 draws ~2,700W—nearly 4× more. AI racks now routinely exceed 50 kW, which air cooling physically cannot handle. Liquid cooling is now mandatory, not optional, creating a massive retrofit and new-build opportunity across the industry.

3. Power is the scarce resource, not capital. The four hyperscalers alone committed ~$650 billion to AI infrastructure in 2025–2026. Yet over 7 GW of planned 2026 US data center capacity has slipped because operators have the money but cannot get the power. Grid interconnection queues run 4–7 years. Transformer lead times stretched to 2–4 years. This scarcity is itself a signal: whoever secures reliable power access has a durable moat.

Key Inflection Point The AI race has become an energy race. Microsoft's Three Mile Island deal, Amazon's investment in X-energy, and Google's Kairos Power agreement are not energy policy decisions — they are competitive strategy.

Evidence

Three Data Points That Support the Thesis

Data Point 1 — The Spending Curve Broke Upward

$582B

Global data center systems spending is forecast to reach $582 billion in 2026, up from $236 billion in 2023. That $346 billion increase arrived in three years — it took the prior twelve years to grow from $140B to $236B.

NVIDIA's data center revenue tells the same story from the demand side: 65× growth in six years, from $2.98B in FY2020 to $193.7B in FY2026, including 68% growth in the most recent fiscal year.

Sources: Gartner (spending forecast); TechnologyChecker / NVIDIA public filings (revenue). Growth rates: +39.4% in 2024, record +48.8% in 2025, forecast +31.7% in 2026.

Data Point 2 — CapEx Commitments Are Still Accelerating

+56% Upward Revision

Between August 2025 and February 2026, analyst expectations for spending by the 14 largest public data center developers in FY2027 climbed 56%. The capex of these firms is tracking close to $750 billion in 2026 alone, vs. ~$450 billion in 2025.

The sector is expected to nearly double in capacity between 2025 and 2030, adding ~97 GW and creating an estimated $1.2 trillion in real estate asset value — with tenants spending an additional $1–2 trillion on IT fit-out.

Sources: BloombergNEF (March 2026); JLL 2026 Global Data Center Market Outlook.

Data Point 3 — Power Demand Is a Grid-Level Event

1,000 TWh by End-2026

The IEA projects global data center electricity consumption will exceed 1,000 TWh by end of 2026 — equivalent to Japan's entire annual electricity usage. In the US, data center energy demand is projected to nearly double from 80 GW (2025) to 150 GW by 2028.

The PJM capacity market price for 2026-2027 jumped to $329/MW — over 10× the $29/MW cleared for 2024-2025 — with data center growth identified as the primary driver. This is not a demand forecast; it is a market price signal that has already cleared.

Sources: IEA Energy & AI Report; Bloom Energy (Jan 2026); PJM capacity market data.

Risk Assessment

Two Risks That Could Undermine the Thesis

High Probability

⚡ Execution Slippage

As of April 2026, only ~one-third of data centers planned for 2026 completion have broken ground. The resulting 7 GW shortfall is real and growing. Grid interconnection queues exceed 2,100 GW — more than total US grid capacity. Transformer manufacturers cannot be scaled up overnight.

The risk: hyperscalers have committed capital for assets that will be delayed 12–36 months. This creates a near-term overcapacity risk in GPU cloud supply (neocloud companies are particularly exposed) even as demand for physical infrastructure remains acute.

Medium Probability

🏛️ Regulatory & Political Backlash

Data centers have become political lightning rods from both sides. PJM projects a 6 GW reliability shortfall by 2027. Utility rate hikes of $31B were requested in 2025 alone. Virginia residents now pay higher electricity bills to subsidize hyperscaler campuses consuming 25% of state power.

Texas SB-6 (2025) added new cost and disclosure requirements. If the cost-shifting narrative hardens into legislation nationally, permitting timelines extend further — compounding the execution risk above. This is a slow-burn risk, not an immediate catalyst.

Conclusion

Bottom Line — Six Lines

The AI infrastructure buildout is the largest capital formation event in technology history, and it is still accelerating. The constraint has migrated from chips to power, turning utilities, transformer makers, and liquid cooling specialists into unexpected beneficiaries. Hyperscaler CapEx commitments have been revised up 56% in six months — capital formation is still expanding, not contracting. The 7 GW execution gap and 4–7 year interconnection queues create real near-term friction, but they also raise barriers to entry and reward whoever secures power access early. This is a decade-long structural theme with a near-term execution risk overlay — the power layer is where the asymmetric upside now sits. Watch cooling, grid infrastructure, and nuclear more than the GPU layer itself.

  • Power is the new GPU — access to reliable electricity is the structural moat of the 2020s
  • Liquid cooling spending will compound as rack densities cross the 50 kW threshold universally
  • Inference growth = permanent, recurring infrastructure demand, not a one-time training spend
  • Execution slippage creates a monitoring opportunity — delays ≠ demand destruction
  • Nuclear and baseload energy deals are competitive strategy, not ESG optionality
  • Regulatory/political risk is real but slow-moving — a 2027–2028 concern, not 2026
